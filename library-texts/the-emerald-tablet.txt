THE EMERALD TABLET (TABULA SMARAGDINA)
=========================================================================

The Emerald Tablet is a short Hermetic text attributed to Hermes Trismegistus. Its earliest
known Arabic versions date to the 6th-8th centuries CE. Latin translations appeared from the
12th century onward. It became one of the foundational texts of Western alchemy and Hermeticism.
The original language is disputed; the text was likely composed in Arabic and perhaps translated
from a lost Greek source.

=========================================================================
VERSION 1: NEWTON'S TRANSLATION (c. 1680, unpublished)
=========================================================================

[Isaac Newton's translation of the Emerald Tablet, from his unpublished manuscript
(Keynes MS 28), written approximately 1680. First published in the 20th century.
Newton's manuscript is in the public domain.]

Tis true without lying, certain and most true.
That which is below is like that which is above and that which is above is like that which
is below to do the miracles of one only thing.
And as all things have been and arose from one by the mediation of one: so all things
have their birth from this one thing by adaptation.
The Sun is its father, the moon its mother,
the wind hath carried it in its belly, the earth is its nurse.
The father of all perfection in the whole world is here.
Its force or power is entire if it be converted into earth.
Separate thou the earth from the fire, the subtile from the gross sweetly with great
industry.
It ascends from the earth to the heaven and again it descends to the earth and receives
the force of things superior and inferior.
By this means you shall have the glory of the whole world and thereby all obscurity shall
fly from you.
Its force is above all force. For it vanquishes every subtle thing and penetrates every
solid thing.
So was the world created.
From this are and do come admirable adaptations, whereof the means (or process) is here
in this.
Hence I am called Hermes Trismegist, having the three parts of the philosophy of the
whole world.
That which I have said of the operation of the Sun is accomplished and ended.

VERSION 2: THE LATIN VERSION (c. 12th century, translator unknown)
=========================================================================

[This is the standard Latin version as it appears in medieval manuscripts and as translated
into English. The Latin text itself was first published in the 12th century and is well out
of copyright. The English rendering below follows the standard 19th century scholarly
translations, which are also PD.]

Verum, sine mendacio, certum et verissimum:
[True it is, without falsehood, certain and most true:]

Quod est inferius est sicut quod est superius, et quod est superius est sicut quod est
inferius, ad perpetranda miracula rei unius.
[That which is below is like that which is above, and that which is above is like that which
is below, for the performing of the miracles of the One Thing.]

Et sicut omnes res fuerunt ab uno, meditatione unius, sic omnes res natae fuerunt ab hac
una re, adaptatione.
[And as all things were from One, by the mediation of One, so all things arose from this
One Thing by adaptation.]

Pater eius est Sol, mater eius Luna, portavit illud Ventus in ventre suo, nutrix eius
Terra est.
[Its father is the Sun, its mother is the Moon; the Wind has carried it in its belly; its
nurse is the Earth.]

Pater omnis telesmi totius mundi est hic. Vis eius integra est, si versa fuerit in terram.
[The father of all Telesma of the whole world is here. Its power is integral if it be
turned towards the earth.]

Separabis terram ab igne, subtile a spisso, suaviter cum magno ingenio.
[Thou shalt separate the earth from the fire, the subtle from the gross, gently and with
great skill.]

Ascendit a terra in coelum, iterumque descendit in terram, et recipit vim superiorum et
inferiorum.
[It ascends from the earth to the heaven, and descends again to the earth, and receives the
force of things superior and inferior.]

Sic habebis gloriam totius mundi. Ideo fugiet a te omnis obscuritas.
[Thus thou wilt have the glory of the whole world. All obscurity will flee from thee.]

Haec est totius fortitudinis fortitudo fortis, quia vincet omnem rem subtilem, omnemque
solidam penetrabit.
[This is the strongest of all powers, the force of all forces, for it overcometh every
subtle thing and doth penetrate every solid substance.]

Sic mundus creatus est.
[Thus was the world created.]

Hinc erunt adaptationes mirabiles, quarum modus est hic.
[Hence will there be marvellous adaptations achieved, of which the manner is this.]

Itaque vocatus sum Hermes Trismegistus, habens tres partes philosophiae totius mundi.
[Therefore am I called Hermes Trismegistus, having the three parts of the philosophy of the
whole world.]

Completum est quod dixi de operatione Solis.
[What I have said of the operation of the Sun is complete.]

VERSION 3: JABIR IBN HAYYAN VERSION (8th-9th century Arabic; English trans. PD)
=========================================================================

[This is the version attributed to Jabir ibn Hayyan (Geber), from the Book of the Secret
of Creation (Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa), attributed to Apollonius of Tyana. The Arabic original
dates to approx. 650-750 CE. This English rendering follows 19th/early 20th century
scholarly translations of the Arabic text, which are PD.]

This is the truth: the certain, without doubt, the true.

What is the above is from the below, and the below is from the above. The work of wonders
is from one.

And all things sprang from this essence through a single projection. How marvellous is
its work! It is the principle part of the world and its custodian.

Its father is the sun and its mother the moon. The wind bore it in its body, and the
earth nourished it.

The father of talismans and the keeper of wonders is here.

And it has power over those which are perfect, and those which illuminate by the fact that
it descends to earth, and separates the earth from the fire.

Thou shalt separate the earth from the fire, and the rarified from the gross, prudently
and with skill.

And it ascends from earth to heaven, so that it acquires the force of things above and
below.

By this means thou shalt obtain the glory of the whole world, and all darkness shall
flee from thee.

This is the strong power of all power, because it will overcome all subtle things and
penetrate every solid.

So the world was created.

From this will arise marvellous adaptations, which are here set out.

Accordingly I am Hermes, who has three parts of the wisdom of the whole world.

And what I have said about the Sun's work is complete.

VERSION 4: TRITHEMIUS VERSION / ORTHOLANUS COMMENTARY EXTRACT (PD)
=========================================================================

[From the commentary of Ortholanus (Hortolanus), written c. 1350, widely published in
the 15th-16th centuries. This is in the public domain by age. The Ortholanus gloss was
included on the sacred-texts.com page and is reproduced here.]

Ortholanus saith upon this: The Philosopher, wishing to recapitulate what he had said, says,
I am called Hermes, for I am wise in philosophy. "Having three parts of the philosophy
of the whole world," i.e., I have treated in the first part of mineral stones; in the second
of vegetable herbs; in the third of animal creatures.

[Note: The actual Ortholanus commentary is lengthy. The sacred-texts.com emerald.htm page
presents multiple versions of the core text. The versions reproduced above (Newton, Latin,
Jabir) represent the primary PD translations included on that page.]

NOTES ON THE TEXT AND ITS HISTORY
=========================================================================

The Emerald Tablet is one of the most famous texts in the Western hermetic and alchemical
tradition. Key facts:

1. EARLIEST SOURCES: The text first appears in Arabic in the Book of the Secret of Creation
   (Kitab Sirr al-Khaliqa), falsely attributed to Apollonius of Tyana, probably composed
   c. 650-750 CE. Another early Arabic version appears in the Book of the Composition of
   Alchemy (Kitab al-Kimya) attributed to Jabir ibn Hayyan.

2. LATIN VERSIONS: The earliest Latin translations date to the 12th century (Hugh of Santalla's
   translation, c. 1140). The standard Latin text was incorporated into many alchemical
   compilations and commentaries through the medieval period.

3. MODERN SIGNIFICANCE: The phrase "As above, so below" is commonly derived from this text
   (though that exact wording is a later paraphrase).

4. NOT INCLUDED: The 2006 Doreal translation (published by Brotherhood of the White Temple) is
   a 20th-century copyrighted work and has NOT been included here, per library guidelines.

5. NEWTON'S TRANSLATION: Isaac Newton's translation was among his private alchemical
   manuscripts (Keynes MS 28, now at King's College Cambridge). It was first published in
   the 20th century and is in the public domain.
